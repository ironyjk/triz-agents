# Genrich Altshuller and the Development of TRIZ

## The Inventor of Invention

Genrich Saulovich Altshuller (1926-1998) was a Soviet engineer, inventor, and science fiction writer who created TRIZ — the Theory of Inventive Problem Solving (Teoriya Resheniya Izobretatelskikh Zadach). His central insight was radical: inventive creativity is not a gift but a skill that can be learned, taught, and systematized.

## Early Life and the Patent Revelation

Born in Tashkent, Uzbekistan SSR, Altshuller showed inventive talent early — he received his first patent at age 14. After serving in the Soviet Navy as a patent examiner, he began systematically studying patents in the late 1940s. Where others saw individual inventions, Altshuller saw patterns.

His key observation: the same inventive principles appeared across unrelated fields and decades apart. An engineer solving a problem in metallurgy would independently rediscover the same solution pattern that a chemist had used 30 years earlier. If these patterns could be extracted and codified, inventors wouldn't need to wait for inspiration.

## Arrest and the Gulag Years (1950-1954)

In 1950, Altshuller wrote a letter to Stalin criticizing the state of Soviet invention and proposing reforms. He was arrested, charged with "inventor's subversion," and sentenced to 25 years in the Vorkuta labor camp. During his imprisonment, he continued developing TRIZ — reportedly working through problems mentally and discussing ideas with other imprisoned intellectuals.

He was released in 1954 after Stalin's death. The gulag years, rather than breaking him, solidified his conviction that systematic methods could replace trial-and-error in invention.

## Building the TRIZ Framework

### The 40 Inventive Principles (1956-1969)

Altshuller's first major publication, "On the Psychology of Inventive Creativity" (1956), introduced the concept of inventive levels and contradictions. Through analysis of 40,000 patents, he identified 40 recurring principles that inventors used to resolve contradictions. These became the foundation of TRIZ.

### The Contradiction Matrix (1969-1971)

To make the 40 principles actionable, Altshuller created the 39×39 Contradiction Matrix. It maps "improving parameter" versus "worsening parameter" to the most frequently successful principles. This was the first practical tool that let engineers look up solution strategies instead of brainstorming blindly.

### ARIZ — Algorithm of Inventive Problem Solving (1959-1985)

ARIZ is Altshuller's most ambitious creation: a step-by-step algorithm for solving inventive problems. It went through multiple versions (ARIZ-61, ARIZ-71, ARIZ-77, ARIZ-85B, ARIZ-85C), each more refined. ARIZ-85C, the final version, remains the most comprehensive TRIZ tool, guiding the solver through problem reformulation, contradiction analysis, resource identification, and solution generation.

### Evolution Patterns (1975-1985)

By studying how technological systems change over time, Altshuller identified 8 patterns of evolution — predictable directions that all technologies follow. These patterns enable technology forecasting: predicting the next generation of a product or system before the market demands it.

## The TRIZ Community

Altshuller trained thousands of students through seminars across the Soviet Union starting in the 1960s. He established TRIZ schools in Baku (Azerbaijan), where he lived from 1990 until his death. Many of his students became TRIZ masters and carried the methodology worldwide after the fall of the Soviet Union.

## Global Spread (1990s-Present)

After the dissolution of the USSR, TRIZ practitioners emigrated to the United States, Europe, Israel, South Korea, and Japan. The methodology was adopted by major corporations:

- **Samsung** — integrated TRIZ into its product development process in the early 2000s, training over 50,000 engineers. Samsung credits TRIZ with contributing to multiple breakthrough products.
- **Intel** — used TRIZ for semiconductor manufacturing problem solving.
- **Boeing** — applied TRIZ to aircraft design and manufacturing.
- **LG, Hyundai, POSCO** — major Korean adopters.
- **Procter & Gamble, General Electric** — applied TRIZ across product lines.

Software tools like IHS Goldfire (now part of Elsevier), CREAX, and TriSolver made TRIZ principles accessible through searchable databases and guided workflows.

## Legacy and Continuing Development

Altshuller died on September 24, 1998, in Petrozavodsk, Russia. His work has been extended by followers in several directions:

- **TRIZ+** — Integration with other methodologies (Six Sigma, Lean, DFSS)
- **OTSM-TRIZ** — Nikolai Khomenko's extension for non-technical problems
- **Systematic Innovation** — Darrell Mann's modern classification (2,500+ case studies)
- **MATRIZ** — The International TRIZ Association, which certifies TRIZ practitioners at 5 levels

The patent database that Altshuller originally studied (40,000 patents by hand) has been expanded by modern researchers to over 2.5 million patents analyzed by software, confirming and extending his original findings.

## Why TRIZ Matters Today

Altshuller's core thesis — that inventive solutions follow patterns, and those patterns can be taught — has proven remarkably durable. In an era of AI-assisted design, TRIZ provides something that neural networks alone cannot: a structured reasoning framework that explains *why* a solution works, not just *what* the solution is. TRIZ does not replace creativity; it removes the randomness from it.

> "Every problem has already been solved. The question is whether you know where to look."
